Lower-risk Conditions

Most people can work safely.

Review the tips below and on OSHA's Heat Illness campaign site: Actions for Lower Risk Conditions.

Water and Shade

  • Adequate amounts of drinking water must be in convenient, visible locations close to the work area.
  • Drink plenty of water, even if you are not thirsty.
  • If in the heat less than 2 hours and involved in moderate work activities, drink 1 cup (8 oz.) of water every 15 to 20 minutes.
  • During prolonged sweating lasting several hours, drink sports drinks containing balanced electrolytes.
  • Generally, fluid intake should not exceed 6 cups per hour.
  • Provide shade, hats, and sunscreen, and encourage their use.

Emergency Planning and Response

  • Check weather forecast regularly and make sure plans are in place before hot outdoor work begins.
  • Make sure medical help (clinic, hospital, emergency services) is nearby—if not, first aid must be performed on site.
  • Appropriately trained personnel and adequate medical supplies must be available.
  • Know where you are in case you need to call 911.

Work and Rest

  • Acclimatize new and returning workers. These individuals may be at high risk for heat-related illness, even at a lower heat index.
  • See moderate-risk precautions if working in direct sun or without a light breeze.
  • See high-risk precautions if wearing heavy or non-breathable clothing or impermeable chemical protective clothing.

Training

  • Train workers on how to recognize the signs and symptoms of heat-related illness and what to do in an emergency, risk factors, and prevention.
