Additional Recommendations

Be Prepared for Emergencies

Have a plan in case a worker experiences heat-related illness. Everyone on site should know the procedures for responding to possible heat-related illness:

  • What steps to follow if a worker has signs or symptoms of heat-related illness.
  • Who to call for medical help and how to give clear directions to the worksite if calling 911.
  • Who will provide first aid until the ambulance arrives.

Post signs with the above information at worksites where heat is a hazard in English and other languages of workers.

A written Heat Alert Program (see NIOSH Pub No. 2016-106, Section 6.2.6) should be developed and implemented whenever the weather service forecasts a heat wave is likely to occur.

Provide Training

Train workers before hot outdoor work begins. Tailor training to cover worksite-specific conditions.

Train all workers and supervisors about the following:

  • Recognition of the signs and symptoms of heat-related illnesses.
  • Causes of heat-related illnesses and prevention.
  • Proper care and use of heat-protective clothing and equipment.
  • Risk factors for heat-related illness.
  • The importance of acclimatization.
  • The importance of immediately reporting any symptoms or signs of heat-related illness.
  • Procedures for responding to symptoms of heat-related illness and contacting emergency medical services if needed.

In addition, supervisors should be trained on the following:

  • How to implement appropriate acclimatization.
  • What procedures to follow when a worker has symptoms consistent with heat-related illness, including emergency procedures.
  • How to monitor weather reports.
  • How to respond to hot weather advisories.
  • How to monitor and encourage adequate fluid intake and rest breaks.

Take Time to Acclimatize

  • Gradually increase time working in hot conditions over 7 to 14 days.
  • For new workers, the schedule should be no more than 20% of the usual duration of work in the heat on day 1 and no more than a 20% increase on each additional day.
  • For workers with recent experience, the schedule should be no more than 50% of the usual duration of work in the heat on day 1, 60% on day 2, 80% on day 3, and 100% on day 4.
  • Closely supervise new employees for the first 14 days or until they are fully acclimatized.
  • Non-physically fit workers require more time to fully acclimatize.
  • Acclimatization can be maintained for a few days of non-heat exposure.
  • Taking breaks in air conditioning will not affect acclimatization.

Stay Well Hydrated

  • Employers should provide the means for appropriate hydration.
  • Water should be clean, less than 15°C (59°F), and made accessible near the work area.
  • Estimate how much water will be needed and decide who will obtain and check on water supplies.
  • Encourage workers to hydrate themselves.
  • If in the heat less than 2 hours and involved in moderate work activities, drink 1 cup (8 oz.) of water every 15–20 minutes.
  • During prolonged sweating lasting several hours, drink sports drinks containing balanced electrolytes.
  • Individual, not communal, drinking cups should be provided.
  • Avoid alcohol and drinks with high caffeine or sugar.
  • Generally, fluid intake should not exceed 6 cups per hour.

Watch for Signs and Symptoms of Heat-related Illness

  • Check routinely to make sure workers are making use of water and shade and not experiencing heat-related signs and symptoms.
  • Monitor new workers and unacclimatized workers more closely.
  • Implement a buddy system in which workers are responsible for observing fellow workers for early signs of heat-related illness.
  • For workers wearing heavy or non-breathable clothing or during extreme heat conditions, check for physical signs (e.g., body temperature, heart rate) of possible over exposure to the heat.

Take Time to Rest and Cool Down

  • Permit rest and water breaks when a worker feels heat discomfort.
  • Modify work/rest periods to give the body a chance to get rid of excess heat.
  • Assign new and unacclimatized workers lighter work and longer, more frequent rest periods.
  • Shorten work periods and increase rest periods:
    • As temperature, humidity, and sunshine increase.
    • When there is no air movement.
    • If protective clothing or equipment is worn.
    • For heavier work.
